## Purpose

This document defines how the house behaves in Kaegan.exe.

The house is not just the setting.

The house is the main threat.

The house performs the story through doors, sounds, lighting, notes, drawings, impossible routes, blocked paths, false safety, and repeated nightmare loops.

The player should feel like the house is aware of them.

## Core Rule

The house controls the rules.

The house decides:

- when the player can move
- when the player can sprint
- when a door opens
- when a door refuses to open
- when a room is safe
- when a hallway changes
- when a sound repeats
- when a note appears
- when a drawing becomes important
- when the player is allowed to continue

The house should not feel random.

The house should feel intentional.

## The House Performs The Story

The house should tell the story through behavior instead of exposition.

The house should use:

- sound before explanation
- light before instruction
- notes before dialogue
- drawings before cutscenes
- doors before tutorials
- route changes before direct commands
- silence before scares

The house should rarely tell the player what to do directly.

The player should learn by noticing what the house changes.

## Guidance Rule

If the house wants the player to move, it should guide them through environmental behavior.

The house can guide the player by:

- turning on a light
- turning off a light
- opening a door
- closing a door
- placing a note
- placing a drawing
- repeating a sound
- making a path feel unsafe
- blocking the expected route
- leaving only one path available

The guidance should feel like the house is manipulating the player.

The player should not feel like they are following a normal objective marker.

## Denial Rule

The house can deny the player access to expected safety.

The strongest denial points are:

- bedroom door locking after it was safe
- hallway refusing to lead to Dad's room
- familiar rooms becoming inaccessible
- doors opening only after a drawing is collected
- safe paths becoming unsafe after a false wake

The house should not deny every route constantly.

Denial should matter when it happens.

## Door Rule

Doors are one of the house's main tools.

A door can be:

- a boundary
- a warning
- a trap
- a threshold
- a memory point
- a progression gate
- a scare surface
- a false promise of safety

Doors should not only be functional objects.

Doors should carry emotional weight.

The player should learn that opening a door means accepting the house's next rule.

## Light Rule

Lighting should be used as communication.

The house can use light to:

- reveal a note
- hide a doorway
- imply a route
- make a room feel watched
- make a safe room feel unsafe
- partially reveal Bad Dream Mommy
- make the player question what they saw

Lighting should not fully explain the scene.

Light should reveal just enough to create dread.

## Sound Rule

Sound is the house's first language.

The house should use sound before visual scares whenever possible.

Important house sounds include:

- dishes crashing
- carpet-drag footsteps
- SHHFFFT doorway movement
- doors slamming
- distant floor pressure
- house settling sounds
- crutch clinks
- prosthetic leg clicks
- slow weighted steps
- silence after sound

The player should learn that sound usually means the house has changed something.

## Silence Rule

Silence is not empty.

Silence means the house is listening.

Silence should happen after important events.

Use silence after:

- dishes crash
- footsteps approach
- Bad Dream Mommy disappears
- a note is collected
- a door slams
- a false wake begins
- the Dad Mimic rhythm stops

The player should become uncomfortable when the house becomes quiet.

## Drawing And Note Rule

Drawings and notes are not normal collectibles.

They are progression keys, haunting triggers, and story fragments.

A drawing or note can:

- unlock a door
- trigger a sound
- change a hallway
- start a false wake
- reveal a memory fragment
- prove that an event happened
- force the player to turn around
- move the player deeper into the house

Notes should be short.

Drawings should carry emotional meaning.

Neither should over-explain the entire story.

## House Memory Rule

The house remembers incorrectly.

The house may repeat:

- sounds
- room layouts
- hallway patterns
- note locations
- drawings
- doorway behavior
- movement restrictions
- familiar routes

But repeated events should not be identical.

Each repetition should feel slightly changed.

The house should feel like it remembers the shape of a memory but not the meaning.

## Impossible Space Rule

The house can change space.

The house may:

- stretch a hallway
- replace a room with a wall
- make a door lead somewhere wrong
- make the bedroom unreachable
- make Dad's room impossible to reach
- open new routes only after denial
- change a path when the player turns around

Impossible space should be used with intention.

The player should feel like the house is choosing the route.

## False Safety Rule

The house should offer safety and then take it away.

Examples:

- the bedroom feels safe until the door-face event
- Dad's room feels like safety but cannot be reached
- waking up feels safe until something has changed
- quiet feels safe until the next sound happens
- a familiar route feels safe until it becomes impossible

The player should learn that safety inside the house is temporary.

## False Wake Rule

A false wake is not a restart.

A false wake is an escalation.

After a false wake, at least one thing should change.

Possible changes:

- a new note appears
- a door is locked
- a hallway is longer
- a sound is closer
- lighting changes
- Bad Dream Mommy behavior changes
- Dad Mimic sounds begin
- a familiar route no longer works

The player should feel relief first, then dread.

## Escalation Rule

The house escalates by changing behavior, not by only getting louder.

Escalation can come from:

- less control
- longer silence
- closer sounds
- blocked routes
- impossible spaces
- repeated notes
- stronger doorway events
- Dad Mimic rhythm appearing
- familiar rooms becoming wrong

The house should not spam jump scares.

The house should make the player anticipate them.

## Restraint Rule

The house should show less than the player wants to see.

The house should not explain too much.

The house should not make every room dangerous.

The house should not make every sound loud.

The house should not reveal every entity clearly.

Fear needs quiet space around it.

## First Build Use

For the first playable build, the house only needs to prove a small set of behaviors:

- lock player movement at start
- trigger dishes crash
- pause in silence
- trigger carpet-drag footsteps
- show the door-face event
- unlock player movement
- require first drawing interaction
- open bedroom door
- stretch or fake the hallway
- deny Dad's room with a wall
- display the turn-around note
- slam and lock the bedroom door
- open another path

This is enough to prove the house can perform the story.

## Do Not Do

Do not make the house feel random.

Do not use objective markers as the main guidance system.

Do not explain every event.

Do not overuse jump scares.

Do not make every door scary.

Do not make every note long.

Do not let combat solve anything.

Do not let the player defeat the house.

## Guiding Statement

The house is frightening because it knows the shape of safety and uses it incorrectly.

It remembers.

It redirects.

It denies.

It waits.

It gives the player control only so it can change what control means.
